Phase 1, Data Foundation: Universe, Cleaning

I collected 11+ years of daily prices for the whole S&P 500, cleaned them into one gap-free table, and cut each company's history into comparable 60-day snippets, the raw material every later phase uses.

What this phase does

Download daily prices for every S&P 500 company, tidy them into a single aligned table with no missing days, and reshape each company's returns into a stack of 60-day (~3-month) snippets, all put on the same scale so later steps compare the shape of behaviour rather than how big or pricey a stock is.

A problem I hit and how I fixed it

Fourteen single-day price moves were larger than 50%. Rather than assume they were data errors, I checked each one, they were genuine market events (for example PG&E's 2019 wildfire-liability crash), so I kept them. Nothing was silently thrown away.

Phase 2, VAE Training

A small neural network learned to turn each stock's 60-day behaviour into a compact latent representation (a short numerical summary of its behaviour), trained only on the early years and then frozen.

I cut each stock's history into 60-trading-day chunks, which is about three months, one quarter. I needed fixed-length pieces because the neural network has to compare like with like: every stock's behaviour has to be described over the same window length so the summaries are directly comparable. Sixty days is the sweet spot, long enough to capture a real medium-term pattern of how a stock moves, but short enough that I still get plenty of chunks per stock to train on and to test on later.

What this phase does

Train a VAE (a type of neural network) on 2015–2017 data so it can summarise any stock's 60-day pattern as a short latent representation. The model is then frozen, never changed again, so that when I later apply it to 2020+ data, it has genuinely never seen those years. Finally I inspect the latent representations to confirm they are meaningful.

What I achieved

  • A trained, frozen model that rebuilds unseen patterns well.
  • The make-or-break check passed strongly: stocks with close latent representations really do move together (agreement +0.48), and it is not luck (p = 0.001).
  • Textbook look-alikes land as near-neighbours, JPMorgan & Bank of America, Coca-Cola & Pepsi, Visa & Mastercard.

A problem I hit and how I fixed it

Two of the twelve latent dimensions came out essentially blank, the same value for every stock, carrying no information. The fix is simple: I leave those two out in the next phase so they cannot add noise to the grouping.

Phase 3, HDBSCAN Clustering and Stability

HDBSCAN stands for Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise, a method that automatically finds dense clusters of similar stocks and leaves the ones with no clear peers unlabelled, without being told in advance how many groups to look for.

I grouped the stocks by their latent representations into behaviour groups, a clean utilities group, a banks group, a property-firms group, and so on, and confirmed the groups are real by re-doing the grouping a hundred times.

What this phase does

Automatically sort the 80-odd most distinctive stocks into behaviour groups, without fixing in advance how many groups to expect, and set aside any stock with no clear look-alike. Then test how dependable those groups are.

What I achieved

  • Groups with an obvious, sensible story, e.g. a 23-stock utilities group, a 24-stock banks group, and a property-firms group.
  • Very high stability: re-doing the grouping on 90% of the stocks 100 times gives an average match score of 0.86 (1.0 would be identical every time).
  • 8 solid groups covering 80 stocks pass forward to the pair search.

Phase 4, Cointegration and Pair Selection

Inside each group I tested every possible pair to see whether its price gap reliably springs back, double-checked the strong ones with a second test, and trimmed down to a tradeable shortlist.

What this phase does

For every pair of stocks within a group, measure whether their price gap is springy (it stretches, then reliably pulls back), how fast it springs back, and the balancing weight needed to make the pair market-neutral. Keep only the strong, fast-enough ones.

Key outputs

First chart (four panels). The gap over time for the four strongest pairs, shown as 'how unusual is it right now'. The red dashed lines mark the unusually-stretched levels where I would open a trade. What I want to see is the line wandering up and down across the middle and repeatedly poking past the red lines, each poke is a trading opportunity, so a good pair gives me plenty.

How to read the gap line (and which stock to short)

Each panel is a single line, the gap between the pair's two stocks, not the two stocks drawn separately. The line is essentially stock A minus a balanced amount of stock B (A is asset1, B is asset2 in the table), so its height measures how far ahead A is relative to B. The vertical axis is "how unusual is the gap right now", measured in steps (standard deviations) from the pair's own normal gap.

  • Line at 0 (the middle): the gap is at its usual size, the two stocks are at their normal distance. Nothing to do.
  • Line high (up at +2): A has run ahead of B (A relatively expensive, B relatively cheap).
  • Line low (down at −2): A has fallen behind B (A relatively cheap, B relatively expensive).

The sign of the line tells me which stock to short. I always short the one that has run ahead and buy the one that has lagged, betting the gap closes:

Line positionWhat it meansShortBuy
At +2 (top red line)A is relatively expensiveShort A (asset1)Buy B (asset2)
At −2 (bottom red line)B is relatively expensiveShort B (asset2)Buy A (asset1)

Example (NTRS = A, RF = B). If the line shoots up to +2, NTRS has got expensive relative to RF, so I short NTRS and buy RF, betting NTRS comes back down. If it drops to −2, NTRS is cheap relative to RF, so I buy NTRS and short RF. Either way the bet is the same, the gap closes, and I close the trade when the line returns to the middle.

Direction from the sign, size from the hedge ratio. The sign of the line tells me which stock to short; the balancing weight (hedge ratio) tells me how much of B to trade against each unit of A, so the two legs offset each other and the position barely moves with the overall market.

What each column in the shortlist means

ColumnWhat it means and represents
asset1, asset2The two stocks that make up the pair. I buy asset1 and short asset2, so the profit comes from the gap between them closing, not from the market.
eg_pvalueMy main test for whether the pair's gap reliably springs back to normal instead of drifting away for good. The score says how confident I can be that this is real and not luck: lower is stronger, and every pair here is below 0.05 (less than a 5% chance of being a fluke). It is the gatekeeper, a pair only reaches the shortlist if it passes.
half_lifeThe spring-back speed, roughly how many days the gap takes to close half of a stretch. I keep only 5 to 60 days, fast enough to trade but not just noise.
spread_stdHow wide the gap typically swings. It has to be at least 0.01, otherwise the gap is too small to trade profitably.
clusterWhich Phase 3 behaviour group the pair came from. Both stocks always sit in the same group, so I only ever pair genuine look-alikes.
johansenA second, independent test that asks the same "does the gap spring back?" question a different way, used as a cross-check. True means it agrees. When both tests agree, I am much more confident the pair's relationship is genuine and stable enough to trade, not a quirk of one method.

What's next (Phases 5–10)

The first four phases above build and validate the engine. The remaining phases, beginning with the trading strategy, put it through its paces and write it up. Here is what each one covers.

Phase 5, The trading strategy

Trade the shortlisted pairs out-of-sample on 2020+ data: a single strongest pair to show the mechanics, then an evenly-weighted, market-neutral basket of ten pairs.

Phase 6, Walk-forward test

Re-run the whole pipeline on a rolling schedule across 2020+: every few months, re-group the stocks, re-find the pairs and trade the next stretch, using the same frozen encoder. This produces one continuous, realistic out-of-sample track record, the headline result of the dissertation.

Phase 7, Pair persistence

Track which pairs survive from one period to the next and which come and go, and see whether pairs change more during market stress.

Phase 8, Trading costs

Apply realistic commission and slippage to every trade and check whether the strategy still makes money after fees.

Phase 9, Benchmark comparison

Run the exact same pipeline with simpler engines, a basic linear version, a plain price-correlation version, and a buy-and-hold baseline, to prove the machine-learning approach actually adds value.

Phase 10, Results & write-up

Compile the final figures, tables and summary statistics into the dissertation chapters.

Glossary

Pairs trading
Buy one stock and short another that normally moves with it; you profit when the gap between them returns to its usual level, regardless of which way the market goes.
Latent representation (latent code)
A short list of numbers (here, 12) summarising how a stock has recently behaved. Look-alike stocks get similar latent representations.
VAE (variational autoencoder)
The small neural network that produces the latent representations. It learns to squeeze a 60-day pattern down to 12 numbers and rebuild it, arranging the numbers so 'close together' means 'behaves alike'.
Behaviour group / cluster
A set of stocks with similar latent representations, found automatically rather than by industry label.
Cointegration ('springy' gap)
Two stocks' price gap reliably springs back to a normal level instead of wandering off, exactly what makes a pair tradeable.
Hedge ratio / balancing weight
How much of stock B to short against one unit of stock A so the pair ignores the overall market's direction.
Half-life (spring-back speed)
Roughly how many days the gap takes to close half of a stretch.
Z-score / 'steps'
How unusual today's gap is versus its recent normal, in standard deviations. I open past 2 and close near half a step; this automatically widens in turbulent markets and tightens in calm ones.
Sharpe ratio
Reward for the bumpiness endured, higher is better; above about 1 is considered good.
Drawdown
The worst drop from a high point to a later low along the way.
Beta
How much something moves when the whole market moves 1%. Near zero means market-neutral.
Match score (ARI)
How closely two groupings agree: 1 = identical, 0 = no better than chance. Used to check the groups are stable.
Out-of-sample
Data the model never saw while being built (here, 2020 onward), the fair test of whether it really works.
